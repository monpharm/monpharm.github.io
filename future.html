What's Next?

After the discovery and preclinical research of our new drug, the next step will be to perform studies on humans (i.e. clinical trials). The basic goal of clinical trials is to test the effectiveness, dosage, and safety of a new drug in humans. Clinical trials always start after receiving the approval from the community of health authority and ethics in the country or area where the drug is registered. Before the start of a clinical trial, researchers need to design a protocol which includes the major research questions and aims. The protocol will be developed by both the drug researcher and manufacturer and must be fully understood and signed by participants (i.e. informed consent) (1). Clinical trials usually have four different phases, which are phase I to IV.

Clinical Trial Phases

Phase I Trial

First in-human trial with a small group of healthy volunteers or patients with disease. The number of people in a Phase I trial is usually between 20 to 100. The period of study will be several months. Major purposes of Phase I trials include safety, dosage evaluation and adverse effects identification.

Phase II Trial

Purpose of a phase II trial is to identify efficacy and common side effects in the patient group. Patient group size will be up to several hundreds of people. In phase II trials, the drug will usually be tested against a placebo control group. This process will take from several months to at least two years.

Phase III Trial

Only a few novel drugs will pass phase II and approach phase III trial. This is the final confirmation of drug efficacy and safety, monitor side effects reactions and compare to common therapies in a long-term period. This phase will take more than a year to at least 4 years. Around 1000 to 3000 patients will be selected in this phase.

Phase IV Trial

The last phase of a clinical trial. It is a postmarked study by monitoring safety, optimal use, benefit, and risk after being sold to the general patients. This phase will keep ongoing during the drugâ€™s lifetime.

After Clinical Trials

Approval

Approval by the national drug community is important to give a novel drug a legal identification. Clinical trials need to be approved in order to begin. Clinical trials may be stopped or delayed because of any failure. Drug approval community such as Food and Drug Administration (FDA) in the USA, Therapeutic Goods Administration (TGA) in Australia.

Post Marketing Monitoring

After being marketed to the general patients, post market monitoring is important. Drug advertising may affect the sales of the drug and further affect the benefit of the manufacturer. Manufacturer inspection is to ensure the drug always be produced under criterion. Intellectual property (IP) situation of the drug is important to prevent generic drugs, it helps to protect the manufacturer's benefit.
